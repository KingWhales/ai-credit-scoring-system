## Research Relevance

Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) have significantly transformed decision-making processes across numerous industries, including healthcare, manufacturing, cybersecurity, and financial technology. In the financial sector, AI-driven predictive models are increasingly being adopted to automate risk assessment, improve operational efficiency, and support data-driven lending decisions. Credit scoring remains one of the most important applications of predictive analytics, as it enables financial institutions to estimate the likelihood of loan repayment and manage credit risk effectively.

Traditional credit scoring systems primarily rely on historical credit records, repayment histories, and financial statements. Although these approaches have proven effective for applicants with established borrowing histories, they often disadvantage individuals and small businesses that possess limited or nonexistent credit records. As a result, many financially responsible applicants are excluded from accessing credit despite demonstrating stable financial behavior through income consistency and spending patterns.

Recent advances in machine learning have introduced the possibility of utilizing alternative behavioral indicators, including transaction frequency, income stability, and expenditure regularity, to complement traditional financial variables. At the same time, the increasing use of AI in financial decision-making has raised concerns regarding transparency, fairness, and accountability. Regulatory bodies and financial institutions increasingly require explainable AI techniques capable of providing understandable justifications for automated decisions.

Consequently, there is a growing need for intelligent credit assessment systems that not only achieve high predictive accuracy but also provide transparent, explainable, and inclusive decision-making capabilities. Addressing these challenges is essential for promoting financial inclusion while maintaining responsible lending practices.

## Degree of Study of the Problem

Credit risk assessment has been extensively studied within finance, statistics, and computer science. Traditional statistical approaches, particularly logistic regression and discriminant analysis, have long served as the foundation for credit scoring models used by financial institutions.

With the advancement of artificial intelligence, researchers have explored machine learning algorithms such as decision trees, random forests, support vector machines, gradient boosting methods, and neural networks for predicting loan default risk. These models often demonstrate superior predictive performance compared to conventional statistical techniques.

Recent studies have also investigated the use of alternative financial data and behavioral indicators to improve credit assessment for individuals lacking extensive credit histories. Simultaneously, explainable artificial intelligence techniques such as SHAP and LIME have gained attention for increasing transparency and interpretability in financial prediction models.

Despite these advances, existing studies often focus on isolated aspects of the problem, such as algorithm comparison, explainability, or feature engineering, without integrating them into a complete software platform capable of supporting real-world loan management processes. Furthermore, relatively few studies combine alternative behavioral indicators with explainable machine learning within a deployable end-to-end application.

This dissertation seeks to address these gaps by designing and implementing an integrated AI-powered credit scoring and loan management system that combines predictive analytics, explainable AI, and software engineering principles into a unified solution.
